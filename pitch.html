Get Time Back

Subtract logistics so presence can grow.
Working prototype · v0

Most productivity tools help you do more. This one does the opposite: it reads a messy day, finds the time hidden inside it, and points that time back at the things you actually care about — then defends it so work can't quietly refill the gap.

The problem

A to-do list lies about time. A 15-minute errand isn't 15 minutes — it's the travel, the waiting, and the focus it shatters on either side. The cost is invisible, so it never gets managed, and presence erodes one logistics task at a time.

The insight

Estimate a task's true cost, route it to the one move that shortens it, and treat reclaimed time as something to protect — not a vacuum to refill. Efficiency that isn't defended just becomes a faster treadmill.

What it does
  1. Estimates true time — travel, waiting, and the focus a task fragments around it, not just its duration.
  2. Learns your reality — corrects toward what tasks actually took, learning recurring items precisely and new ones from the category average.
  3. Coordinates the household — turns "pick up the kids" into a carpool swap with a free, willing family member, behind a consent gate.
  4. Acts only on your say-so — proposes reversible moves (block, draft, delegate, delay-start); nothing happens until you confirm.
  5. Defends the time it frees — spends the reclaimed budget on what you named matters, and fences those blocks so nothing else lands there.
Why it's hard to copy

The household graph

A shared family time-map — who's free, who can drive, who's opted in — lets it coordinate across people, where the biggest savings actually live. A personal-productivity app can't easily build that.

The defend-the-time loop

Reclaimed time gets a named destination and is protected. That loop — efficiency in service of presence — is the whole product, and the thing every other tool stops short of.
